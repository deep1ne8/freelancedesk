The Freelance Rate Calculator: Stop Guessing What to Charge

Published 2026-07-17 · 2 min read · pricing

You are not bad at pricing. You're guessing.

Most freelancers set their rate by copying a number they saw online, or by what feels "reasonable." That's not pricing — it's roulette. The result is predictable: you're either leaving 30% on the table or pricing yourself out of the room, and you have no idea which. A rate calculator ends the guessing by working backwards from what you actually need to live.

The formula nobody taught you

Your minimum rate isn't what the market will bear — it's what your life costs, converted into billable hours. Reverse it:

  1. Monthly need = living expenses + business costs + taxes set-aside (about 20–30% of take-home).
  2. Annual need = monthly need × 12.
  3. Billable hours = hours/week you can actually sell × weeks you work (not 52 — subtract vacation, sick, admin).
  4. Minimum rate = annual need ÷ billable hours.

Example: $3,200/mo need × 12 = $38,400. At 25 billable hrs/week × 46 weeks = 1,150 hrs. Minimum = $33/hr. Anything below that, you're subsidizing the client. Charge it knowingly or don't.

Why "billable hours" is smaller than you think

The trap is using 40 hours. You don't bill 40. You bill the slice clients pay for; the rest goes to invoicing, finding work, and learning the tool. A freelancer working "full time" often has 20–28 truly billable hours. Use that number or the calculator lies to you.

Add the scope-creep surcharge

Messy clients cost more. Bump your quote 15% per risk level: clean project +15%, average +30%, chaotic +45%. This isn't greed — it prices the emotional labor of managing a disorganized client.
