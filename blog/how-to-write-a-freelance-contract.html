How to Write a Freelance Contract That Actually Protects You

Published 2026-07-17 · 2 min read · contracts legal

The most expensive mistake freelancers make isn't a bad client

It's starting work before the paperwork is signed. Every freelancer who has been ghosted, stiffed, or scope-creeped into oblivion can trace it back to one moment: they began the work because the client "seemed nice." A contract isn't paperwork for lawyers — it's the price of admission. No signed agreement, no project.

What a real freelance contract actually needs

You don't need a 40-page legal monster. You need six clauses that cover the situations where money and trust break down. Here they are, in plain English.

  1. Scope, written tightly. "Design a website" is a lawsuit waiting to happen. "Five-page marketing site, two revision rounds per page, copy provided by client" is a project. Vague scope is where 90% of disputes live — spend the sentences here.
  2. Payment terms with a deposit. Net 14, a 30–50% deposit before you start, and a late fee of 1.5% per month. The deposit filters tyre-kickers and funds your first week so you're never working for free.
  3. A kill fee. If they cancel after you start, you get 50% of the remaining value plus everything already logged. This single clause ends most "actually we're pausing" ghosting.
  4. IP transfers on payment, not before. You own what you make until the invoice is paid in full. An unpaid client has no license to use your work. This is your only real leverage.
  5. Revision limits and change orders. The price buys N rounds. Anything extra is a written change order at your rate, agreed before you do it. This is how you kill scope creep.
  6. Termination + confidentiality. Either side can exit with 7 days' notice; both keep each other's secrets for two years. Boring, but it protects you if the relationship sours.

The email that closes the loop

Send this the moment a client says "let's do it":

"Great — I'll send over the contract and a deposit invoice (30%) to get us started. Once that lands, I'll lock in your start date. The full agreement is short and protects both of us — happy to walk you through any clause."

If a prospect pushes back on a contract, that's data. The clients who respect a simple agreement are the ones who pay on time.

Build the habit, not the document

One contract, reused with the brackets filled, beats a perfect template you never send. Keep it in your toolchain so it's the default, not the exception. The free invoice generator handles the money side; the contract handles the trust side. Do both, every time, and you'll out-earn freelancers who "just started."
