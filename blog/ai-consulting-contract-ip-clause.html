The AI-Output IP Clause Every Consulting Contract Needs

Published 2026-07-19 · 3 min read · contracts

Who actually owns what an AI produces for a client?

Most freelance contracts say something like "all work product developed for the client is hereby assigned to the client." That sentence is fine for a hand-coded website. It is a quiet legal trap the moment an AI tool is involved. The model weights belong to OpenAI, Anthropic, Meta, or whoever trained them. You did not create them, you cannot assign them, and pretending otherwise is the fastest way to get a clause thrown out — or to breach a warranty you signed. An AI-output IP clause exists to say, plainly, which rights move and which ones only get licensed.

The three layers of "IP" in an AI deliverable

Treat an AI-assisted deliverable as three separate things, because the law treats them differently:

  • Your own code and writing. Anything you typed by hand, or substantially rewrote and arranged, is yours to assign. This is the normal "work made for hire" transfer and it works exactly as your old contract expects.
  • The raw model output. The text, image, or code a model generated on its own. Under current US Copyright Office guidance, purely machine-generated output with no human creative control is not copyrightable at all — so there is literally nothing to assign. You grant the client a broad, perpetual license to use it instead.
  • The model and its weights. Never assignable by you, whether it is a closed API or an open-weight model like Llama or Mistral. With open-weight models you can use the outputs commercially, but you still cannot claim ownership of the weights themselves. The client gets a license to the output, never the engine.

A clause you can actually drop in

Here is a plain-English starting point. Adapt it; don't paste it verbatim into a binding agreement without a lawyer:

1. Provider assigns to Client all right, title, and interest in human-authored work product.

Then handle the machine part separately, because it is a different animal:

2. AI-assisted output is licensed to Client under a perpetual, irrevocable, worldwide license to use, modify, and sublicense. Provider does not transfer ownership of any third-party model or its outputs.

Don't forget the prompts and the data

Your prompts are often your real competitive edge — they encode your methodology, your guardrails, and years of taste. Decide up front whether they stay confidential to you or get handed to the client. Same for client data fed into a model: your clause should state that training or input data remains the client's, is not used to improve third-party models, and is deleted on request. A one-line promise that you will not paste the client's confidential data into a public chatbot without permission saves you from a nightmare later.

The warranty that protects you back

Clients will ask you to warrant that deliverables don't infringe third-party IP. Make that warranty narrow and honest:

  • You warrant human-authored portions are original and cleared to assign.
  • You warrant you are licensed to use the AI tools you employed under their terms.
  • You do not warrant that AI output is free of any third-party resemblance — no one reasonably can — but you agree to fix or replace flagged portions at no charge.

When the client brings their own model

If the engagement runs on the client's enterprise API key or a private deployment, the IP analysis flips. The output is theirs from the start, and your role is integration, evaluation, and review. Say so explicitly: "Where Client-supplied models generate output, Client retains all rights; Provider assigns any human-authored integration code." This avoids the awkward situation where you've "assigned" something you never owned in the first place.

The five-second test

Before you sign, ask one question: does this clause distinguish between what I created, what the model created, and the model itself? If all three are lumped into one "assign everything" sentence, rewrite it. Clear IP terms are not about squeezing the client — they are what let both sides actually use what was paid for, and what keep a routine engagement from turning into a dispute about who owns the deliverable.
